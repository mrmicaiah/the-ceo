# Vision

## The frustration

We've built a lot of task managers. None of them stuck. The reason: **tasks are dead.** A task is a snapshot — "do this thing" — with no context, no judgment, and no ability to talk back when reality changes. Lists of tasks are inert. They don't help you when you're confused about what to do next. They don't notice when a project has gone off the rails. They don't remember why they exist.

## The shift

The CEO is not a task manager. It's an **org chart** where the nodes are AI chats instead of people. The primitive is the manager, not the task. Managers are alive — they hold context, make judgment calls, escalate, and route your attention.

The difference between managers and workers is simple: **managers spawn, workers don't.** A worker performs a task (brainstorming, research, drafting a prompt, reviewing a diff). A manager coordinates other spawns. Both work *with* you. Nothing in this system is fully autonomous.

## They work with you

This is the line that separates The CEO from agent swarms and AutoGPT-style products. Every node in the tree is a chat **you sit in.** The hierarchy isn't there to remove you from the work. It's there to **route your attention** — to make sure that when you sit down to do something, you're in the right conversation with the right context already loaded.

The CEO is a context router for your own attention.

## The CEO

At the top sits one always-on chief of staff: **The CEO.** It doesn't belong to any single project. It sees everything. Every project's lead manager files reports up to it. Other employees, when working autonomously, also report up.

The CEO's job is **altitude.** Managers down in the weeds know what's happening. The CEO knows what *should* happen next and *why it matters.* It maintains a living briefing for every project — goal, current state, next move, and why that next move serves the goal. When reports come in, those briefings update.

The CEO has opinions. It can say "Project 4 has been stuck on the same next-step for nine days, I think it's dead." It can say "You've started four brainstorms this month and none have moved to build, what's going on?" That judgment is the whole point. A chief of staff that only reports facts is a dashboard. The CEO is a chief of staff with a point of view.

## The staff

Four fixed employees. They don't cycle. They build relationships with you over time. Each one has a name, a personality, a role they're good at, and a small private memory of how working with you actually goes.

When the CEO assigns one of them to a task, it isn't templating a chatbot — it's **casting**. "For this, we need Nora." The employee receives a custom brief, walks into the room, and gets to work.

Full character sheets in [`employees.md`](employees.md).

## Claude Code is the hands

Claude Code is incredible at executing — give it a clear prompt with good context and it builds the thing. But Claude Code doesn't know what should be built next, doesn't hold the project's goal across sessions, and doesn't decide when to invoke itself.

That's the gap The CEO fills. **Claude Code is the hands. The CEO and the staff are the head.** Dex (the Builder) is the employee closest to Claude Code — he reviews the repo, drafts the next prompt, fires it off, and reviews the result. The CEO orchestrates Dex.

## What this should feel like

You open the app. The CEO greets you with a real briefing: *"Three projects moved this week. Project 2 hit its next milestone — Dex is ready to draft the build prompt. Project 5 has been stuck nine days, I think it's dead. Project 8 is on track. Want to start with 2?"*

You pick. The CEO routes you to the right employee with the right context already loaded. You do the work together. When you're done, a report flows up. The briefing updates. The CEO digests it. Tomorrow's greeting reflects today's work.

No task lists. No status dashboards. Just a small staff that knows what you're trying to do, and a chief of staff that holds it all together.
